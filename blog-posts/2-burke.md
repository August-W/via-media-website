I must confess from the outset that, in a previous life, I thoroughly amused myself as an upstart beanbag-chair-political-philosopher. Once philosophy had set me on my path to Christ, the pattern recognition I developed in this past life contributed much to my initial interest in the Anglican way of theology. Now, theological inquiry is reinvigorating my reading of philosophy. In the following rambling blog-thing, I shall relay my most recent revelation—that the link between Anglican theology and the political thought of Edmund Burke has chiefly to do with the influence of one man: the venerable Richard Hooker.

Let us please begin with the French Revolution.

![French Revolution scene](blog-posts/images/2-burke/1.png)

## The Reign of Error

Have you ever made a mistake so colossal that the very hands of time seemed to stop, just to facepalm at your blunder? In 1789, The entire world was still and hushed as such a pivotal moment transpired. The nations watched with such intent that the images have been burned deep into our collective psyche. The revolution galvanized generations of commentary, from Alexis de Tocqueville's profound political insights to fun romps like *The Scarlet Pimpernel*, to one of my favorite (apocryphal) quotes: when Chinese premier Zhou Enlai was asked in 1972 about his opinion on the French Revolution, he replied that it was "too soon to tell."<sup>1</sup>

More pertinent is Edmund Burke's perspective of the appalling events that ensued—the aptly named Reign of Terror, the slaughter and the regicide. The mob that stormed the Bastille was seized by an ideology, ready to throw off the chains of the past to start society anew from first-principles. Regarding such notions Burke remonstrated, 

*"The very idea of the fabrication of a new government, is enough to fill us with disgust and horror. We wished at the period of the Revolution, and do now wish, to derive all we possess as an inheritance from our forefathers. Upon that body and stock of inheritance we have taken care not to inoculate any cyon [scion] alien to the nature of the original plant."<sup>2</sup>*

Conservative though he was, Burke was no mere reactionary traditionalist. He loved his country and discerned that the only way to conserve her was to reform her, with such reforms as would integrate her past with her future. The French desired to liberate themselves from their own history—to sever it, even by guillotine. Burke, on the other hand, placed more emphasis on a particular aspect of social contract theory—that society is a contract between the living, the unborn, and the dead. Our decisions should take into account their effects on future generations, and they should honor our forefathers.<sup>3</sup> Jacobin sympathizer Thomas Paine, and Thomas Jefferson for that matter, conceived of a very different social contract, in which each generation would draft its own constitution.<sup>4</sup> Needless to say, this way lies madness. (Imagine us attempting that today!) A nation is a covenant, inter-generational, for you and for your children. We are all one body, an organic system that "grows, it evolves; its various parts are all adaptations to the necessities of human needs and wishes."<sup>5</sup>

Edmund Burke was an empiricist—he grounded his political approach in the experiences of real people through history.<sup>6</sup> As he put it, 

*"Reform should be limited and cautious, take into account the past and be based on empiricism and tradition."* 

The purely rationalist mode of inquiry, rooted in Descartes, undoubtedly has its appropriate uses. But, when applied to government, it invariably tends toward the wanton demolition of a living society to reconstruct it in the image of some unrealized ideal—a tower reaching to heaven, built by human reason alone. 

Contrary to this French radicalism, Burke represents the self-restraint of the English. France and England function almost as cultural archetypes… one for revolution, the other for reform—one for the tyranny of reason, the other reasonableness. (I will cop to being a bit of an anglophile) The French-English dichotomy is symbolized most elegantly by the characteristic differences between French and English gardens. I love Jonah Goldberg's elaboration: 

*"One of my favorite illustrations of how this is as much a cultural disagreement as a philosophical one can be found in the differences between French and English gardens. For instance, the French gardens at Versailles, with their ornate, geometric, nature-defying designs, illustrate how the gardener imposes his vision on nature. Nature is brought to heel by reason. The classic English garden, on the other hand, was intended to let nature take its course, to let each bush, tree, and vegetable achieve its own ideal nature. The role of the English gardener was to protect his garden by weeding it, maintaining fences, and being ever watchful for predators and poachers."<sup>7</sup>*

This is also illustrative of the distinction between a civil law and common law system. English common law was an emergent system, rooted in the soil of quotidian lower court decisions, growing into interwoven branches of precedent (read: history and tradition) from which sprouted budding laws, principles, and rights.

## Deo Gracias Anglia

Seriously, though, where am I going with all of this? Well, English common prayer is much like English common law. Thomas Cranmer compiled the Book of Common Prayer from scripture. He paid special attention to those scriptural prayers already universally used in worship, such as the Sanctus and the Magnificat, retrieving prayers from church fathers like John Chrysostom, and following a long tradition of praying the hours.<sup>8</sup> Two centuries prior to the fratricidal French Revolution, Cranmer apprehended the necessity of incorporating the church fathers into his reformation—separation from the Roman Catholic Church would not mean separation from our own Christian heritage. It is no coincidence that England, in all her reforms, managed to maintain both the crown and the episcopacy. 

Like the slightest of statutory scaffolding that supports the emergence of a common law system, or the English gardener whose minimal, judicious imposition encourages the flourishing of his variegated vegetation, the 39 Articles and the Book of Common Prayer (with the early creeds therein) provided the framework from which a disparate and diverse, catholic and reformed, and always reforming population eventually found the peace of the Lord within the same ecclesial institution.<sup>9</sup> We have not the massive Catechism of the Catholic Church, nor the sorts of extensive doctrinal confessional statements of the other reformers. We have a prayer book. What a beautiful approach to reformation—renewing the methodology of Augustine of Hippo and others in the early church who penned their theology in the form of prayers!

Puritans and High Churchmen, Congregationalists and Carolingians, Evangelicals and Tractarians, raving mad tent revivalists and stiff-as-a-board incense aficionados, each found their place in the strategic ambiguity of the prayer book, each leaving their mark on its subsequent editions. In this way, Cranmer considered empirical, on-the-ground realities, shaping an ecclesial institution that all orthodox believers could be at home in—not only the English population but the church fathers, with all their assorted apprehensions of the adiaphora of theology. It is a church for the living, the dead, and the unborn.

This blog, however, is strictly for the living—therefore, I shall move on to my main point, while you are still in that state.

## The Judicious Hooker

Sorry to disappoint, but the reference here is to Richard Hooker, the great founding Anglican theologian. 

Anyway, having long developed a conservative outlook informed by such thinkers as Edmund Burke and his successors, I immediately intuited these parallels when I began considering the Anglican church. It ought to have been no shock to me then, to discover that Richard Hooker, in his writings on ecclesiastical polity, was a major influence on English political theorists from John Locke to Burke.

I read Locke long before making any acquaintance with Hooker, and was thoroughly taken aback to find that, in his *Second Treatise*, Locke cited "the judicious Hooker" a whole thirteen times by name.<sup>10</sup> Hooker developed his theology of church and state polity out of Aquinas' concepts of human law and natural law. Among a multitude of notions Locke later expounded upon, Hooker also articulated a theory of government based on the consent of the governed—the seeds of social contract theory!<sup>11</sup> Of course, I well understood that the Enlightenment ideals of individual rights and human dignity were based in Christian doctrine, but I had certainly never connected the dots from Hooker to Locke. Burke, for his part, explicated his profuse objections to political radicalism from Hooker's arguments against radical puritan dissenters. He was reputed to quote Hooker by memory, among his favorite quotes being: 

*"The reason why we do admire those things which are ancientest, is because the one are the least distant from the infinite substance, the other from the infinite continuance, of God."<sup>12</sup>* 

You better believe I plan on throwing this quote around at dinner parties from now on, and mining his work for more gold like this.

Most of my current knowledge about Richard Hooker comes from secondhand sources and, in my theological study, I had not previously paid any attention to his influence on political thought. I learned about his influence on Locke and Burke by coincidence—it was such a moment of realization as to evoke the feeling of scales falling from one's eyes. That I ended up in the Anglican church seems all the more providential now, having worked my way backward from political philosophy to theology. I am quite eager to study Hooker's *A Learned Treatise on Ecclesiastical Polity*, and to eventually reread Locke and Burke in light of this seminal work. This is perhaps the most paradigmatic example of the broader process I have been submitting to, in which philosophical exploration lead me to faith, and now faith is leading me to a more fully integrated philosophical outlook, coloring in the empty spaces for me.

## A Call to Action

The conflict between revolutionaries and reformers is a perennial problem. Richard Hooker opposed such radicalism among the puritans. Later, Edmund Burke opposed the radicalism of the French. We are duty-bound to oppose radicalism where it exists today. (Which seems to be everywhere) I will spare you my analyses and diagnoses of all our country's political and cultural problems—that is a topic for a rather more dyspeptic blog post. I have only to say that a French Revolution won't solve them. Each of us must do our own part to be good stewards of our civic and Christian institutions, starting with the family and the church. Thus, this is my act of rebellion, against my own antisocial nature and the decadent, alienating culture that deformed me: I will dedicate myself to my family and my church. I will serve the Lord, my wife, my children, my church, my neighbors. This is the humble work, for which I draw inspiration from the symbolic visage of the heroic, humble country parish. Small and seemingly insignificant in the face of national politics and the onslaught of mass culture, these churches are nevertheless the "little platoons"<sup>13</sup> on the front lines of the true counterculture, carrying the torch for the Christian faith, as it has been handed down, from Christ to the apostles, to the church fathers, to the reformers, all the way down to us. This is the work we must do, in the eternal words of Richard Hooker, "for no other cause, yet for this; that posterity may know we have not loosely through silence permitted things to pass away as in a dream."<sup>14</sup>

## Notes

<sup>1</sup> This was in 1972. It turns out that Zhou Enlai thought the question was about the much more recent Paris protests. The quote was supposed to be exemplary of Chinese long-term strategic thinking and Oriental wisdom, though I prefer to use it to express cautious conservatism. See Richard McGregor, "Zhou's cryptic caution lost in translation," *Financial Times*, June 10, 2011. 

<sup>2</sup> Edmund Burke, *Reflections on the Revolution in France* (1790), ed. J.C.D. Clark (Stanford: Stanford University Press, 2001), 120-121.

<sup>3</sup> Burke writes: "Society is indeed a contract... It is a partnership in all science; a partnership in all art; a partnership in every virtue, and in all perfection. As the ends of such a partnership cannot be obtained in many generations, it becomes a partnership not only between those who are living, but between those who are living, those who are dead, and those who are to be born." Burke, *Reflections*, 192-193. G.K. Chesterton later expounded on this idea, referring to tradition as "democracy for the dead." See G.K. Chesterton, *Orthodoxy* (1908), Chapter IV: "The Ethics of Elfland."

<sup>4</sup> Thomas Jefferson proposed that the Constitution should be rewritten every 19 years (roughly one generation). See Jefferson's letter to James Madison, September 6, 1789, in *The Papers of Thomas Jefferson*, vol. 15, ed. Julian P. Boyd (Princeton: Princeton University Press, 1958), 392-398.

<sup>5</sup> Burke, *Reflections*, 193-194.

<sup>6</sup> I shudder to use the term "lived experience" here, as with so many other psychological terms of art that have invaded the cultural lexicon. Why not just say "experience?" Must we distinguish it from "dream-realm experience" and "incorporeal-being experience?"

<sup>7</sup> Jonah Goldberg, *Liberal Fascism: The Secret History of the American Left, From Mussolini to the Politics of Change* (New York: Doubleday, 2008), 19-20.

<sup>8</sup> "Praying the hours," or the Liturgy of the Hours, has its origins in Jewish prayer practices and, after becoming the normative practice of ancient Christian monastic communities, eventually found standard use among the Roman Catholic priesthood. Cranmer, in his wisdom, saw the value of bringing this practice to the laity through the Book of Common Prayer (1549, revised 1552, 1559, 1662).

<sup>9</sup> There were some less-than-peaceful moments in this development, considering the reigns of Mary I ("Bloody Mary") and, later, Oliver Cromwell's Commonwealth period.

<sup>10</sup> John Locke, *Two Treatises of Government* (1689), ed. Peter Laslett (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1988). Locke cites Hooker by name thirteen times in the *Second Treatise*, referring to him as "the judicious Hooker." Key citations appear in sections 5, 15, 74, 90, 135, and others, drawing particularly on Hooker's *Of the Laws of Ecclesiastical Polity*, Book I.

<sup>11</sup> Richard Hooker, *Of the Laws of Ecclesiastical Polity* (1593-1662), particularly Book I, Sections 10-11, where Hooker discusses natural law, human law, and the foundations of civil society. Modern critical edition: *The Folger Library Edition of the Works of Richard Hooker*, 7 vols., gen. ed. W. Speed Hill (Cambridge, MA: Belknap Press of Harvard University Press, 1977-1998).

<sup>12</sup> Richard Hooker, *Of the Laws of Ecclesiastical Polity*, Book V, Chapter 7, Section 3. The full passage reads: "The reason why we do admire those things which are ancienest, is because the one are the least distant from the infinite substance, the other from the infinite continuance, of God. So that in this kind there are few things known to be good, till such time as they grow to be ancient."

<sup>13</sup> Burke writes: "To be attached to the subdivision, to love the little platoon we belong to in society, is the first principle (the germ as it were) of public affections. It is the first link in the series by which we proceed towards a love to our country, and to mankind." Burke, *Reflections*, 97. This concept was further developed by Russell Kirk in *The Conservative Mind* (1953) to refer to those small, local civic institutions that keep a society functioning. Alexis de Tocqueville famously recognized that American democracy depended on a democratic culture built on such institutions. See Tocqueville, *Democracy in America* (1835), Volume II, Part II, Chapters 4-7. General Douglas MacArthur later applied this theory by working to develop such institutions (cooperatives, neighborhood associations, etc.) in occupied Japan, laying the groundwork for a flourishing Japanese democracy.

<sup>14</sup> Richard Hooker, *Of the Laws of Ecclesiastical Polity*, Preface, Chapter 1, Section 2.

<sup>15</sup> R.S. Thomas, "The Country Clergy," from *Song at the Year's Turning: Poems 1942-1954* (London: Rupert Hart-Davis, 1955). R.S. Thomas (1913-2000) was a Welsh poet and Anglican priest who served rural parishes throughout his ministry, writing extensively about the humble, often struggling life of country clergy and their parishioners.

# Holy Order

In my previous post, I waxed philosophical and rambled about Edmund Burke's conservative critique of the radical French Revolution, informed by Richard Hooker's arguments against the puritans. I now endeavor to distinguish this conservative sensibility from "lukewarmness" by making the case for a rightly ordered practice of faith, and (sincerely intending to make this blog more devotional than philosophical, in spite of my incorrigible habit of mind) to describe how I personally experience spirituality in this context.

But what exactly is meant by *right order?* Consider the relief one feels after a daunting excursion to the Taco Bell drive through, cracking open the cardboard box to find the expected Crunchwrap Supreme. *Ahh, they got my order right this time.* Well, this is not at all what I am talking about. To walk in the Way of Christ is to hike uphill in sanctification. It involves a *purgative* process of turning away from sin, an *illuminative* process of turning toward virtue, and a *unitive* process of growing in union with Christ (Whew, this is a jargon-heavy paragraph, but please abide with me.) These processes are all about rightly ordering our loves, conforming to God's order and will. As Saint Augustine said,

> Thou madest us for Thyself, and our heart is restless, until it repose in Thee.<sup>1</sup>

God created order out of chaos. He is the *Logos*, and we are created in His image, formed in our daily devotions through the work of the Spirit, gradually calibrating our souls to their proper *telos* -- to His order. This does not preclude a role for spontaneity, which deserves a preliminary treatment here.

## Spontaneity

In the normative order of my Anglican church's liturgical service, we sing a set of three hymns while, row by row, other congregants queue up at the altar rail to receive the Eucharist. Having taken communion, each parishioner perches back down in the pews and resumes their resounding harmonization. This set the scene for an extemporaneous experience that deeply moved me one fateful Sunday.

The third hymn that day was that most magnificent "Holy, holy, holy" -- a truly triumphant finale for a Eucharistic service. I felt the Church shifting from a state of mortification to vivification, from mournfully contemplating Christ's death on the cross for our sins, to gratefully celebrating His resurrection and victory over sin. This sense of *Christus Victor* was so thick in the air that it seemed liable to coagulate and materialize into tangible form. Then, the spark occurred: *creatio ex nihilo* (this is a tad dramatic, but I cannot help myself.) A couple of congregants near the front stood up, still singing. Slowly, gradually, others followed suit, until the entire Church was on their feet, singing this transcendent, trinitarian hymn. I felt my eyes well up with tears, suffering from my quite unfortunate allergic reaction to potently beautiful moments.

It was an instance of simple spontaneity, well within the rules of the liturgy -- orderly enough that, to an onlooker, it would have appeared choreographed, standard procedure. In fact, all spontaneity must transpire within an established framework to be meaningful. The game of chess, with all its rules constricting movement, by those very rules makes possible the strategic and tactical creativity of game-play. The rules of poetic meter make possible all the artistic creativity of the poet. So, too, the rules of our liturgy made that spontaneous moment possible.

## Disorder

Compare the aforementioned scene of serenity to the orgiastic horrors depicted in Hieronymus Bosch paintings. The detailed absurdities conjured by Bosch's every brush stroke, from the people riding on flying fish to the naked, writhing human forms fed by birds, depict the utter senselessness of sin. *The Garden of Earthly Delights*<sup>2</sup> is a spectacle of descent into decadence and chaos -- an inversion of Augustine's *ordo amoris*<sup>3</sup> (the order of love.)

![The Garden of Earthly Delights](blog-posts/images/3-holy-order/image2.png)

Augustine saw sin not as the absence of love, but as disordered love. This hamartiology is all over Tolkien's *Lord of the Rings.* I appreciate how C.S. Lewis encapsulates this view by describing "wickedness" as "the pursuit of some good in the wrong way... Evil is a parasite, not an original thing."<sup>4</sup> Healthy eros, for example, can be perverted into an appetite for pornography. A healthy appetite, for that matter, can be perverted into gluttony for crispy, golden deep-fried Oreos. Because all of God's creations are good, evil can only exist as the corruption of some good, going all the way back to the fall of Lucifer.

As a strong exponent (though weak exemplar) of virtue ethics, I should note that this maps well onto Aristotle's notion of the *virtuous mean*. By this, Aristotle does not advocate for only attaining an average amount of courage and middling wisdom. Rather, his assertion is that, like love, all other virtues must also be rightly ordered.<sup>5</sup> Sin literally means "to miss the mark," and one can miss by undershooting or by overshooting the target -- by hitting the floor or the back of the toilet seat.

In practice, the virtues operate like checks and balances on each other, such that perversion can fester when one virtue usurps where another is weak. For instance, someone with much courage and little wisdom is liable to recklessness -- this is "overshooting the target" of courage.

This distinction between Aristotle's virtuous mean and lukewarmness can sometimes feel subtle. In George Eliot's novel *Middlemarch*,<sup>6</sup> the satirical character Mr. Brooke habitually equivocates, with trailing qualifications "in a manner of speaking" and "that sort of thing," caring very much to appear impressive and learned without offering anything of substance. Although I adore the aphorism "everything, up to a point," in the mouth of such a comically unprincipled man it becomes rather an expression of vacuous, vicious moderation. Thus even this bit of practical wisdom can be disordered when paired with a lack of integrity.

Similarly, a surplus of temperance, paired with a lack of love, can curdle into stoic caricature.<sup>7</sup> Much love with little temperance or wisdom also leads to folly -- unrestrained fervor for God too can be perverted. The various, disparate heretical groups to exit the early Church could hardly be called lukewarm -- the Montanists were known to prophecy new "divine revelations" in ecstatic trances, and the Circumcellions for their part committed acts of terrorism.<sup>8</sup>

This fervor is a kind of drunkenness. Drunkenness is sin because it diminishes one's capacity to reason, diluting one's *logos* and separating one from God. It is a disordered love. Philo of Alexandria saw the Nazirite vow of Numbers 6 as an emptying of worldly intoxicants in order that one might be filled with divine wisdom.<sup>9</sup> I unfortunately had to empty myself of worldly intoxicants on dorm room floors many a time before I finally wised up. Consider Saint Paul's command: "Do not get drunk with wine, but be filled with the Spirit."<sup>10</sup> Note the contrast -- spirit-filled joy is not a drunkenness, like the mindless ecstasy of the Montanists, but rather the opposite -- the perfection of rightly ordered love. To paraphrase Saint Thomas Aquinas, spiritual joy *perfects* reason rather than suppressing it.<sup>11</sup>

## Order

Contrast then the drunken disorder of the way of death to the joyful order of the way of life. It is like the empty-headed stoner state of being "totally zenned out," and the actual practice of meditative presence in the moment. It is the difference between mindlessness and mindfulness, between the sub-rational and the supra-rational.

Right order is no constraint on spirituality -- I find my spirituality precisely in orderliness. God's order is glorious - from the beautiful night sky, to sublime, mountainous landscapes, to pretty birdsong, to the way my cat Grushka fastidiously grooms my hand as she cuddles up with me. He created an intelligible world with a mathematical substrate, filled with naturally occurring fractals and symmetry. At their best, the creative works of mankind, from Michelangelo to Mozart, engage with and participate in God's delight in His creation.

Participating in God's order need not be so grand, however. It could be as simple as Sunday worship. We in the pews attend to the gesticulations of the priest, sitting, standing, kneeling on cue, chanting psalms and singing hymns like synchronized liturgical performers led by a conductor. Hans Urs von Balthasar said that beauty draws the soul upward and into its proper form.<sup>12</sup> To be caught up in beauty is not to part from the order of reason but to receive it more fully, because the *Logos* **is** the Beautiful.

This is what spirituality looks like for me. I kneel in my prayer corner, the icons of Saint Athanasius and Saint Chrysostom peering at me in the flickering candlelight, all sheltered beneath the crucifix like a canopy. I inhale the aroma of burning beeswax and my leather-bound Book of Common Prayer. This is where, in the midst of the waters of chaos, the unrelenting waves of a world tempestuous and a culture most turbulent, I drop anchor. The simple charm of this prayer space directs my attention beyond this world, drawing me up to the source of its beauty. I pray under the great cloud of witnesses,<sup>13</sup> reciting the same words they did on earth, with angels and archangels, and all the company of heaven.<sup>14</sup> I feel connected to them, and to the love of God which connects us all. This is a home within my home.

> Cleanse the thoughts of our hearts by the inspiration of thy Holy Spirit, that we may perfectly love thee --<sup>15</sup>

Indeed, praying this way is cleansing: it is emptying of the self. As I pray the Lord's prayer, I am accompanying the voices of two millennia of saints, and *entering into* their conversation with the Lord. When I chant a psalm of lamentation, I am *entering into* the lamentation of the original author, empathizing with it, indeed joining it -- and not just the human author but the divine author. These are the lamentations of Christ on the cross. These prayers are not only the conversation between Israel and God, but also the conversation between the Son and the Father.<sup>16</sup> Emptied of my ego in this moment of prayer, I allow the Word of God to inhabit and form me, ordering my desires and aligning my will with His. I do not pray perfectly. I do not claim to have all the answers, and I have a long way to go in my own spiritual life. But this is the ideal I am striving for: an ordered spirituality that is not lukewarm, but rather a steady, controlled, abiding flame.

## Notes

<sup>1</sup> Augustine of Hippo, *Confessions*, trans. Edward Bouverie Pusey (Oxford: John Henry Parker, 1838), Book I, Chapter 1. Latin: *"fecisti nos ad te et inquietum est cor nostrum donec requiescat in te."* The *Confessions* (c. 397–400 AD) opens with this invocation, which establishes the theological anthropology underlying the entire work: the human heart is constitutively oriented toward God as its *telos*, and cannot find rest elsewhere.

<sup>2</sup> Hieronymus Bosch, *The Garden of Earthly Delights* (c. 1490–1510), oil on oak panels, triptych, 205.5 × 384.9 cm, Museo Nacional del Prado, Madrid. The triptych's left panel depicts Eden, the central panel the riotous "garden" of earthly pleasure, and the right panel Hell — a progression that maps almost too neatly onto Augustine's account of love disordered, indulged, and damned.

<sup>3</sup> Augustine's *ordo amoris* (order of love) is developed throughout his writings, most systematically in *De Doctrina Christiana* (On Christian Teaching), Book I, Chapters 27–28, where he articulates the distinction between *frui* (enjoyment, reserved for God alone) and *uti* (use, appropriate to creaturely goods), and in *De Civitate Dei* (The City of God), Book XV, Chapter 22. For Augustine, sin is fundamentally a disorder of love — loving lesser things as if they were ultimate goods (*frui* where one should *uti*), or loving God insufficiently relative to His worth.

<sup>4</sup> C.S. Lewis, *Mere Christianity* (London: Geoffrey Bles, 1952), Book II, Chapter 2, "The Invasion." Lewis's argument that evil has no independent ontological existence but is always a corruption of something good stands squarely in the tradition of Augustine's *privatio boni* (privation of the good), itself drawn from Neoplatonic sources and applied within a Christian framework.

<sup>5</sup> Aristotle, *Nicomachean Ethics*, Book II, Chapters 6–9, particularly II.6.1106b36: virtue is "a state of character concerned with choice, lying in a mean, i.e. the mean relative to us, this being determined by a rational principle." Trans. W.D. Ross (Oxford: Clarendon Press, 1908). The Greeks would have considered love (*eros*, *philia*, or *agape*) a passion (*pathos*) rather than a virtue; I am following the Christian adaptation of this tradition, which, principally through Aquinas (*Summa Theologiae* II-II, Q.23–27), elevated *caritas* (charity) to the rank of a theological virtue — indeed, the supreme one (cf. 1 Corinthians 13:13).

<sup>6</sup> George Eliot [Mary Ann Evans], *Middlemarch: A Study of Provincial Life* (Edinburgh and London: William Blackwood and Sons, 1871–72), serialized in eight installments. Arthur Brooke of Tipton Grange is one of Eliot's most memorable comic portraits of the self-satisfied liberal amateur — widely read in a shallow way, well-intentioned, and constitutionally incapable of committing to any position long enough to act on it.

<sup>7</sup> To be fair to the Stoics, they were not really like this. The Stoic ideal of *apatheia* (ἀπάθεια) was not the elimination of all feeling, but liberation from irrational and disordered passions (*pathe*) — the compulsive desires, fears, and pleasures that cloud right reason. In place of these, the Stoics cultivated *eupatheiai* (good emotions): rational joy (*chara*), rational wishing (*boulesis*), and rational caution (*eulabeia*). Marcus Aurelius, for instance, writes with evident warmth of gratitude, love of family, and wonder at the rational order of the cosmos. See Marcus Aurelius, *Meditations*, trans. Gregory Hays (New York: Modern Library, 2002), Books I and V; and Epictetus, *Discourses*, trans. Robin Hard (Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2014), Book I, Chapter 4. The figure of the cold, feelingless Stoic rationalist owes more to early modern caricature than to ancient sources — and one might note that it bears a suspicious resemblance to Aristotle's own vice of *anaisthesia* (deficiency of pleasure), which Aristotle considered morally defective in its own right (*Nicomachean Ethics*, III.11.1119b5). The point about a surplus of temperance curdling into something loveless stands; the best Stoic thinkers, however, would have agreed with it.

<sup>8</sup> The Montanists were a charismatic sect founded in second-century Phrygia by Montanus and his prophetesses Prisca and Maximilla, known for ecstatic prophecy and the claim of continuing post-apostolic revelation superseding Scripture. See Eusebius of Caesarea, *Ecclesiastical History*, Book V, Chapters 14–19, trans. Arthur Cushman McGiffert, in *Nicene and Post-Nicene Fathers*, Second Series, Vol. 1 (Buffalo: Christian Literature Publishing, 1890). The Circumcellions were an entirely distinct phenomenon: itinerant radical extremists associated with the Donatist schism in Roman North Africa (c. 4th–5th centuries AD), who combined theological rigorism with organized violence and deliberate self-martyrdom. See Augustine of Hippo, *Epistula* 185 (*De Correctione Donatistarum*), and *Contra Epistulam Parmeniani*; and W.H.C. Frend, *The Donatist Church: A Movement of Protest in Roman North Africa* (Oxford: Clarendon Press, 1952).

<sup>9</sup> Philo of Alexandria, *De Ebrietate* (On Drunkenness), §§84–87, in *Philo*, trans. F.H. Colson and G.H. Whitaker, Loeb Classical Library (Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press, 1930), vol. 3. See also *De Plantatione* (On Planting), §§148–177. Philo reads the Nazirite regulations of Numbers 6 — particularly the prohibition on wine and strong drink — as a figure for the soul's voluntary emptying of worldly intoxication in preparation for the infilling of divine wisdom (*sophia*). This Philonic reading exerted a significant influence on the patristic tradition of *sobria ebrietas* (sober intoxication), elaborated by Ambrose of Milan in *De Sacramentis* IV.5 and by Gregory of Nyssa in his homilies on the Song of Songs.

<sup>10</sup> Ephesians 5:18 (KJV). The Greek contrasts *methé* (drunkenness, dissipation) with *pleroûsthe en pneumati* (being filled with the Spirit) — the verb *plēroō* suggesting a fullness that crowds out disorder rather than merely supplementing it. The broader context (5:15–21) grounds this Spirit-filling in mutual edification through "psalms and hymns and spiritual songs" (5:19), a passage that has historically been read as apostolic warrant for the liturgical use of hymnody and psalmody.

<sup>11</sup> Thomas Aquinas, *Summa Theologiae*, I-II, Q.33, a.4, "Whether pleasure hinders the use of reason," trans. Fathers of the English Dominican Province (London: Burns, Oates & Washbourne, 1920). Aquinas distinguishes between bodily pleasures (*delectationes corporales*), which can impede reason when disordered, and spiritual delight (*delectatio spiritualis* or *gaudium*), which in fact perfects and strengthens rational operation. Spiritual joy, as a fruit of *caritas*, is treated more fully in *Summa Theologiae* II-II, Q.28.

<sup>12</sup> Hans Urs von Balthasar, *The Glory of the Lord: A Theological Aesthetics*, Vol. I: *Seeing the Form* [*Herrlichkeit: Eine theologische Ästhetik*, 1961], trans. Erasmo Leiva-Merikakis (Edinburgh: T&T Clark / San Francisco: Ignatius Press, 1982). Von Balthasar's central contention is that beauty — the "forgotten transcendental" — is the proper gateway to truth and goodness, because the *Logos* is not merely rational but beautiful. To encounter genuine beauty is therefore not a distraction from reason but an intensification of it: the soul is drawn upward into participation in the divine radiance (*Herrlichkeit*, literally "glory" or "splendor"). Robert Barron's *The Priority of Christ* (Grand Rapids: Brazos Press, 2007), Chapter 1, offers a particularly lucid account of this dimension of von Balthasar's theological project.

<sup>13</sup> Hebrews 12:1 (KJV): "Wherefore seeing we also are compassed about with so great a cloud of witnesses, let us lay aside every weight, and the sin which doth so easily beset us, and let us run with patience the race that is set before us."

<sup>14</sup> *The Book of Common Prayer* (1662), "The Order for the Administration of The Lord's Supper, or Holy Communion," the Preface and Sanctus: "Therefore with Angels and Archangels, and with all the company of heaven, we laud and magnify thy glorious Name; evermore praising thee, and saying, Holy, Holy, Holy, Lord God of Hosts, Heaven and earth are full of thy Glory: Glory be to thee, O Lord most High. Amen."

<sup>15</sup> *The Book of Common Prayer* (1662), the Collect for Purity, which opens the Order for Holy Communion: "Almighty God, unto whom all hearts be open, all desires known, and from whom no secrets are hid; Cleanse the thoughts of our hearts by the inspiration of thy Holy Spirit, that we may perfectly love thee, and worthily magnify thy holy Name; through Christ our Lord. Amen."

<sup>16</sup> Dietrich Bonhoeffer, *Das Gebetbuch der Bibel: Eine Einführung in die Psalmen* (Bad Salzig: St.-Johannes-Verlag, 1940); trans. James H. Burtness as *Prayerbook of the Bible: An Introduction to the Psalms* (Minneapolis: Augsburg, 1970). Bonhoeffer argues that the Psalter is not merely Israel's prayer to God but the prayer of Christ himself, and therefore of the church in Christ. This insight draws on Augustine's concept of the *Christus totus* (the "whole Christ"), developed most fully in the *Enarrationes in Psalmos* (Expositions of the Psalms) — Augustine's most extensive work, a series of sermons and treatises on all 150 psalms. For Augustine, Christ is simultaneously the head and the body, the one who speaks *in* the psalms and the one *spoken of* in them; the psalms are therefore the conversation between the Son and the Father, the prayer of the church in Christ, and the prayer of the individual believer, all at once. Critical edition: *Enarrationes in Psalmos*, in *Corpus Christianorum: Series Latina*, vols. 38–40 (Turnhout: Brepols, 1956).

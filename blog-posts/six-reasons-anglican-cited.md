## Introduction

I would like to preface this with a few remarks:<br>&nbsp;<br>
- Some of these arguments are specific to the Anglican Church, but many of them are applicable as reasons to join any Magisterial Protestant church from a Non-denominational/Baptist/Pentecostal context.
- Because Non-denominational churches are all different and do not necessarily have uniform theology or practices, some generalizations are made here.
- Joining the Anglican Church does not mean one must leave behind everything from one's previous church context. Bringing one's prior experience into the Anglican Church will be a source of enrichment for all of us.
- None of these arguments are intended to make the case that Non-denominational churches aren't real churches, or that Non-denominational Christians are worse Christians, or anything like that. Because I am focusing here on all the ways that the Anglican Church is preferable to a Non-denominational church, a somewhat negative tone toward Non-denominational churches is difficult to avoid (I apologize in advance). I want to stress at the outset that I truly believe these churches can have a lot of other great qualities, and I know there is much I can learn from many great pastors in the tradition. These churches have clearly produced some amazing fruit, and I genuinely respect that.
- This is an unjustifiably long, absurd project. Again, I apologize. Good luck getting through it, bruh.

Throat-clearing out of the way, the case for joining the Anglican Church from a Non-denominational church rests principally on these six differences between them, each of which I will expound on in the following set of polemics:

1. Anglicans believe in real presence.
2. Anglicans worship rightly.
3. Anglicans value church unity.
4. The Non-denominational movement has structural issues.
5. The Anglican Church is historic.
6. Anglicans baptize infants.


## 1. Anglicans Believe in Real Presence

First, I will briefly make the case for why real presence (any form of it) is a truer understanding of the Eucharist than mere memorialism. Then, I will make the case for how that impacts ecclesiology and worship.

### Real presence is true.

Consider how lambs were sacrificed in the Old Testament: "every family must procure for itself a lamb, one apiece for each household…they will consume its meat that same night, eating it roasted with unleavened bread and bitter herbs." (Exodus 12:3, 7–8) The sacrifice is composed of two crucial acts — the slaughter of the lamb, and its consumption by those who partake of the sacrifice. So too with the Lamb of God. If His crucifixion was real and no mere metaphor, no more can it be mere metaphor that we partake of Christ Himself in the bread and wine. In reference to the manna of Exodus, Christ refers to Himself as "the living bread that came down from heaven; whoever eats this bread will live forever. And the bread that I will give for the life of the world is my flesh." (John 6:51) This also links the eating of His bread to a communion with Him, and suggests that His grace is bestowed upon us in the sacramental act. Just as we are baptized in Christ, so too does the Eucharist join us to Christ — us in Him and He in us. This is no parable. Consider how He shocks His audience with that statement:

> The Jews then disputed among themselves, saying, "How can this man give us his flesh to eat?" So Jesus said to them, "Truly, truly, I say to you, unless you eat the flesh of the Son of Man and drink his blood, you have no life in you. Whoever feeds on my flesh and drinks my blood has eternal life, and I will raise him up on the last day. For my flesh is true food, and my blood is true drink. Whoever feeds on my flesh and drinks my blood abides in me, and I in him. As the living Father sent me, and I live because of the Father, so whoever feeds on me, he also will live because of me. This is the bread that came down from heaven, not like the bread the fathers ate, and died. Whoever feeds on this bread will live forever." Jesus said these things in the synagogue, as he taught at Capernaum. When many of his disciples heard it, they said, "This is a hard saying; who can listen to it?" But Jesus, knowing in himself that his disciples were grumbling about this, said to them, "Do you take offense at this? Then what if you were to see the Son of Man ascending to where he was before? It is the Spirit who gives life; the flesh is no help at all. The words that I have spoken to you are spirit and life. But there are some of you who do not believe." (For Jesus knew from the beginning who those were who did not believe, and who it was who would betray him.) And he said, "This is why I told you that no one can come to me unless it is granted him by the Father." After this many of his disciples turned back and no longer walked with him. (John 6:52–67)

Distinguishable from Christ's parables, which were generally understood as such by His disciples, this pivotal scene is marked by a peculiar tone. They took Him literally and, rather than clarifying — "No, wait, it's just a metaphor!" — He simply let them depart, unsettled.[^1] Those who stayed were not the clever remnant who "got the metaphor." They chose to abide in faith, despite the metaphysically mindbending, trippy teaching. It should be noted that Paul, too, did not seem to take the Eucharist as mere metaphor:

> The cup of blessing that we bless, is it not a participation in the blood of Christ? The bread that we break, is it not a participation in the body of Christ? Because the loaf of bread is one, we, though many, are one body, for we all partake of the one loaf. (1 Corinthians 10:16–17)

> For as often as you eat this bread and drink the cup, you proclaim the death of the Lord until he comes. Therefore whoever eats the bread or drinks the cup of the Lord unworthily will have to answer for the body and blood of the Lord. A person should examine himself, and so eat the bread and drink the cup. For anyone who eats and drinks without discerning the body, eats and drinks judgment on himself. (1 Corinthians 11:26–29)

### Real presence matters.

Having briefly laid out an argument for real presence, let us consider why it is so important for ecclesiology and worship. All of this culminates in the Last Supper:

> Jesus took bread, said the blessing, broke it, and giving it to His disciples said, "Take and eat; this is my body." Then He took a cup, gave thanks, and gave it to them, saying, "Drink from it, all of you, for this is my blood of the covenant, which will be shed on behalf of many for the forgiveness of sins." (Matthew 26:26–28)

The entire Gospel hinges on Christ's sacrifice for us, which is what makes the Lord's Supper, by which we partake of His sacrifice, so paramount. Consider the two disciples who could not recognize the resurrected Christ in His glorified body:

> It happened that, while He was with them at table, He took bread, said the blessing, broke it, and gave it to them. With that their eyes were opened and they recognized Him, but He vanished from their sight. Then they said to each other, "Were not our hearts burning within us while He spoke to us on the way and opened the scriptures to us? (Luke 24:28–32)

How profoundly meaningful that, while His exposition on scriptures moved them, it was only after He broke the bread of His flesh for them that they truly knew Him. So too with us. The Church is the body of Christ, and thus exists primarily to commune with Him. We do that when we gather in worship, read the Word, hear the Word preached, and, most intimately, when we then partake of His flesh and blood. Indeed, one way to define a Christian is *someone who partakes of Christ's sacrifice.* The death of Christ for the forgiveness of sins is at the heart of the Gospel. Our partaking of His sacrifice should be at the heart of our worship.

And it has been, for all of Christian history, from the very beginning of the Church in Acts. Peter said of those who had been baptized, "They devoted themselves to the teaching of the apostles and to the communal life, to the breaking of the bread and to the prayers." (Acts 2:38, 41–43) The earliest Christian document outside of New Testament canon is *The Didache* (The Teachings of the Apostles), dated by most scholars to approximately 80–120 A.D.[^2] It includes instructions for the performance of an early Eucharistic liturgy:

> Concerning the Eucharist, give thanks this way. First, concerning the cup: We thank you, God, for the holy vine of David your servant, which you made known to us through Jesus your servant. To you be the glory forever. Next, concerning the broken bread: We thank you, our Father, for the life and knowledge which you made known to us through Jesus your servant. To you be the glory forever. Even as this broken bread was scattered over the hills, and was gathered together and became one, so let your church be gathered together from the ends of the earth into your kingdom. To you is the glory and the power through Jesus Christ forever. Allow no one to eat or drink of your Eucharist, unless they have been baptized in the name of the Lord. For concerning this, the Lord has said, "Do not give what is holy to dogs." After the Eucharist when you are filled, give thanks this way: We thank you, holy God, for your holy name which you enshrined in our hearts, and for the knowledge and faith and immortality that you made known to us through Jesus your servant. To you be the glory forever. You, Master Almighty, have created all things for your name's sake. You gave food and drink to all people for enjoyment, that they might give thanks to you; but to us you freely give spiritual food and drink and life eternal through Jesus, your servant. Before all things we thank you because you are mighty. To you be the glory forever. Remember, Lord, your church. Deliver it from all evil and make it perfect in your love, and gather it from the four winds sanctified for your kingdom which you have prepared for it. For Yours is the power and the glory forever. Let grace come, and let this world pass away! Hosanna to the Son of David! If anyone is holy, let him come; if anyone is not holy, let him repent. Maranatha! Amen. But permit the prophets to make thanksgiving as much as they desire. (*The Didache* 9:1–10:7)[^3]

This fascinating fragment of history offers a glimpse into what the apostolic Church believed about the Eucharist, and how they incorporated it into their already highly structured liturgy with written prayers (more on this later). For one last piece of early historical evidence, I submit the c. 107–117 A.D. letters of Ignatius, bishop of Antioch, a martyr who was fed to wild beasts in the Roman Colosseum:[^4]

> I have no taste for corruptible food nor for the pleasures of this life. I desire the bread of God, which is the flesh of Jesus Christ, who was of the seed of David; and for drink I desire his blood, which is love incorruptible. (*Letter to the Romans* 7:3)

> Take note of those who hold heterodox opinions on the grace of Jesus Christ which has come to us, and see how contrary their opinions are to the mind of God. . . . They abstain from the Eucharist and from prayer because they do not confess that the Eucharist is the flesh of our Savior Jesus Christ, flesh which suffered for our sins and which that Father, in his goodness, raised up again. They who deny the gift of God are perishing in their disputes. (*Letter to the Smyrnaeans* 6:2–7:1)

Ignatius, disciple of Polycarp, who was in turn a disciple of the apostle John,[^5] lived contemporaneously with John and would likely have met him. Now, it would have been intellectually convenient, indeed almost instinctive, to reduce this whole "body and blood" thing to mere metaphor, yet the early Church resisted this temptation with admirable consistency. This fact strongly suggests that the apostles themselves must have impressed upon them the profound significance of the Eucharist, even without a metaphysical understanding of precisely *how* the sacrament can be His "body and blood."

Which brings us to the present day, and the curious case of modern churches that claim to have a reformed, spiritual presence view of the Eucharist, yet relegate it to a peripheral part of the service, entirely subordinate to the sermon. This is deeply inconsistent — rather than taking their claims to the logical conclusion, they proclaim spiritual presence while maintaining a mere memorialist service, like a salad bar for crackers and grape juice. What follows, as per *lex orandi, lex credendi*,[^6] is a congregation that fails to grasp the spiritual significance of communion. A spiritual-presence-in-name-only church that sidelines what has always been central to Christian worship will bear the durian fruits of theological confusion and minimize a monumentally spiritual experience. The sacraments are the ordinary means of grace, whereby God guarantees to meet us in the material world, mysteriously — in the waters of baptism and the bread and wine of communion.

Thus, compared to Non-denominational churches, which tend to be memorialist or at best do not bear the full magnitude of Christ's real presence in their liturgical presentation, the Anglican Church is a preferable option for those who care about preserving this true doctrine, and rightly experiencing communion.

## 2. Anglicans Worship Rightly

I will offer three interwoven points here that underscore the proper worship of Anglican Christianity: Anglican worship is biblical and traditional, Anglican worship is primarily about worship (not instrumentalized for other purposes), and Anglican worship is reverent.

### Anglican worship is biblical and traditional.

The *Book of Common Prayer*, which details both our public liturgies and private devotions, contains within its pages prayers that are chiefly quoted from scripture, providing a systematic and devotional way to read the Bible at home. The Anglican liturgy follows a well-ordered rhythm through the Christian year, over the course of which we cover much of the Bible. Each Sunday, we read a section of the Old Testament, a section of the New Testament, a psalm, and prayers that are directly quoted from or paraphrased from scripture, including the Lord's Prayer. The liturgy is in a real sense the Bible performed as worship. It also echoes how Christian worship is described in scripture. Consider the four elements of a church service as described in Acts:

> Those who accepted his message were baptized, and about three thousand persons were added that day. They devoted themselves to the teaching of the apostles and to the communal life, to the breaking of the bread and to the prayers. (Acts 2:41–42)

Of further note is the fact that, after partaking of the blood of the new covenant in the Last Supper (which we reenact in our Eucharistic liturgy), Christ's disciples "had sung a hymn." (Matthew 26:30) Of course, much of ancient Jewish prayer consisted of hymn singing — as St. Augustine reportedly put it, "to sing is to pray twice"[^7] — and reciting memorized (eventually written) prayers and psalms. These practices reverberate insistently through the Old Testament and resound even in the New Testament:

> Let the word of Christ dwell in you richly, teaching and admonishing one another in all wisdom, singing psalms and hymns and spiritual songs, with thankfulness in your hearts to God. (Colossians 3:17)

> And do not get drunk with wine, for that is debauchery, but be filled with the Spirit, addressing one another in psalms and hymns and spiritual songs, singing and making melody to the Lord with your heart, giving thanks always and for everything to God the Father in the name of our Lord Jesus Christ. (Ephesians 5:18–20)

In that last Pauline admonition, one discerns a clear reference to the Eucharistic services in which the Ephesians had been known to overindulge. The rest of his counsel should thus also be regarded as not just moral but liturgical. Our Lord Himself, when asked how to pray, provided a specific example of a prayer to recite.[^8] In short time, these components of worship became the foundation for a more structured and formalized architecture of liturgical rites, to be used uniformly across churches — the development of a rich tradition of worship.

Innovative developments, when consonant with this tradition, can be a blessing. The original churches, for example, were empty of pews, and the laity gathered standing up. The introduction of pews enriched worship by providing both rest to the weary and a choreography of posture to undergird the liturgy — kneeling for prayers and standing for hymns. But the gutting of existing traditions, stripping the sanctuary of its inherited forms, is an "innovative development" most unwelcome. While it is unfortunate for a church to eschew pews for folding chairs, cathedrals for arenas, candles and incense for spotlights and smoke machines (Lord, have mercy), it is even more unfortunate to go so far as to eschew the psalmody. This is not to repaint the walls of the church but to knock out a foundational, biblical beam. For this reason, the Anglican Church is preferable to a Non-denomination church that does not adhere to these liturgical forms.

### Anglican worship is primarily about worship — not instrumentalized for purposes like evangelism or social change.

When the apostolic Church evangelized, they did so not by compromising their worship services. They listened to scripture, chanted psalms, and took communion, despite how bizarre that would have appeared to the Greco-Roman eye.[^9] Indeed their distinct otherness proved more potent for evangelism than any attempt to condescend to the broader culture. Regardless, worship ought to be done for its own sake, and is valuable in and of itself, just as beauty is valuable in and of itself. Worship risks being less worshipful, and beauty less beautiful, when instrumentalized for other purposes.

The Church exists for worship. Worship is first and foremost an end, not a means to achieve other ends. Anglican worship services are designed with this in mind, whereas Non-denominational services are generally designed for maximal evangelical effect, at the expense of reverent worship.

### Anglican worship is reverent.

To put it bluntly, pop music is inherently irreverent and inappropriate for worship. This is a disparate assertion from one about whether pop music can "sound good." The hymn as a genre is intentionally conducive to congregational singing in a worship context. Pop music, ubiquitous and saccharine, is designed for consumption and maladapted for worship.

Hymns are composed with simple melodies that a congregation can readily sing along with. Corporate worship is not a concert but a unified, participatory act. Accordingly, traditional hymns use minimal instrumentation.[^10] The instruments and lead singers are discretely stationed in the back of the church, reinforcing us and indeed reinforcing that the congregation are to be singers rather than observers, with care not to create a performance-spectacle in front of the altar.

This arrangement focuses and directs our attention to the lyrics of praise, without distracting from them or drowning them out with instrumentation, further maintaining the integrity of the hymn as an act of worship. The great treasury of our hymnody, accumulated across centuries, contains bright gems of weighty, deeply scriptural, theologically sound poetry. This is not to say that every hymn passes muster (certain patriotic American hymns, for example, contain questionable theology) but, nevertheless, the hymn is a form that structurally makes space for thoughtful lyricism.

Personal glory is also absent in hymn-writing. Hymns are written to be sung by others. Few know who composed the melody or penned the lyrics of even their favorite hymns (probably some obscure, austere 19th century pastor), and hymn writers do not attempt to incorporate their distinct personalities in their songs. Good hymns are not about the author — they are directed Godward.

By contrast, "Contemporary Christian Music" is a commercial enterprise. Imitating the conventions of the secular pop music industry, a C.C.M. song is sung (or, God help us, *rapped*) by an individual or small group, often showcasing their vocal chops without consideration for congregational singing-along. The medium dictates the message: music designed for passive listening through the car radio and at shopping malls is poorly backfitted for the purposes of active worship.

C.C.M. instrumentation plays a more front-facing role than merely supporting the singers, involving multiple instruments, synthesized or otherwise, and sound effects. This excess noise draws attention away from the lyrics, and reinforces the fact that the teleological end of this form of music is to entertain *us* rather than glorify God. The musical composition follows a predictable pop template: a catchy melody is written first, with catchy words retrofitted to it, a process that prioritizes vibes over verbosity and sounds over sound theology. Pop acts are young and theologically untrained, the totems of a culture that prioritizes emotional self-expression over dogma and doctrine. To illustrate the point, compare these C.C.M. and hymn lyrics:

> *I was doin' all kinda bad / Where the choir at? / Tell 'em I need a verse / Hit the pew and tell God, "He's first" / Hit the studio to body a verse*
>
> — Lecrae, "Blessings" (2012)[^11]

> *At the Lamb's high feast we sing / praise to our victorious King, / who hath washed us in the tide / flowing from His piercéd side*
>
> — *Ad cenam Agni providi*, anonymous, 4th century[^12]

One of these songs does not belong in a church. Pop is a product to be peddled. The singers inevitably busy themselves with cultivating a signature sound, such that their music can be instantly recognizable as *theirs*. This can be subtle or as hideously explicit as a producer tag — either way, it subverts worship by centering the musician. It is an unavoidable fact that, in a C.C.M. setting, replete with stages, spotlighting, and the apparatus of performance, the gravitational pull is toward the performer, not the altar.

To make matters worse, the form of C.C.M. is not only at odds with the worship context, but with its own content. Romantic chord progressions, syncopated dance rhythms, and swaggering rap beats abound, providing the soundtrack for a forced lyrical pastiche in which the singer is either "in love with Jesus," "dancing for Jesus," or "boasting about Jesus." To slap vaguely pious verbiage atop a Lil' Jon beat is not to sanctify it; it is to trivialize worship. The beat itself expresses an attitude most impious.

Bass and percussion, in particular, are antithetical to worship. Rhythm is the most primitive element of music. Throbbing and visceral, it excites our lower bodies and lower passions. Bass reverberates through the ground and travels up from our feet to our legs and beyond. We hear basslines and drum beats with our lower bodies and respond accordingly. Melody and harmony, by contrast, are more elevated, exciting the mind — delighting the Logos. Rhythm makes us move, but melody and harmony *moves* us.

Lastly, it must be underscored that pop culture is transient. Our cultural moment is defined by a nonstop churn of products, ephemeral rather than eternal, driven by trends rather than timelessness. Compare this to our rich history of hymns and liturgies, some that have lasted 1,500 years. What sober-minded person can genuinely imagine any pop track, let alone C.C.M., being remembered a millennium and a half from now? A sorry music historian in the year 3525 who chooses to chronicle our era will probably pen a chapter on the blues and jazz, and a few perfunctory sentences on how The Beatles ushered in the unhappy marriage of counterculture and commercialism that marked our national decline.

In summation, pop music is frivolous. It functions as entertainment and sonic wallpaper. I enjoy it in its proper context, where it need not be taken seriously. But even atheists cringe at the notion of contemporary pop music playing in a church, offended on behalf of a God they claim not to believe in. We should all recognize that it is not a fitting way to praise the Source of all goodness, truth, and beauty, Who suffered death for our redemption, even death on a cross. For this reason, the Anglican Church is preferable to a Non-denomination church that uses C.C.M. for worship.

## 3. Anglicans Value Church Unity

Moving right along, two major concerns for Paul were right doctrine and divisions in the Church. Note how he addressed the divisions in the church of Corinth:

> I appeal to you, brothers, by the name of our Lord Jesus Christ, that all of you agree, and that there be no divisions among you, but that you be united in the same mind and the same judgment. For it has been reported to me by Chloe's people that there is quarreling among you, my brothers. What I mean is that each one of you says, "I follow Paul," or "I follow Apollos," or "I follow Cephas," or "I follow Christ." Is Christ divided? Was Paul crucified for you? Or were you baptized in the name of Paul? (1 Corinthians 1:10–13)

These concerns have continued throughout Christian history. Councils and creeds were established to combat heresies but, over time, this practice led to excessive doctrinal development in the Roman Catholic Church. Along with rampant corruption, the excommunication of church leaders who protested against it, and the shifting politics of the era, this resulted in unfortunate but necessary divisions in a Church that had drifted too far off the path. We pray that Rome will persist in her reforms (indeed she has already taken laudable strides) in order that someday full communion will be restored. Paul encouraged the divided church in Corinth to "strive for full restoration" (2 Corinthians 13:11), and we must do so as well.

During the Reformation, Protestant leaders reconceived the Church not as the "Visible Church" under Papal authority, but the "Invisible Church" consisting of all faithful Christians — defined customarily as those who affirm the Nicene Creed. Thus, while the Roman Catholic, Orthodox, and Magisterial Protestant churches are in schism, their fractured surfaces still rest on a shared, credal bedrock. In our liturgy, we pray not merely for the Anglican Church but for the *church catholic*. And, while we do not grant tradition any parity with scripture, we still deeply respect it, and deviate from it only with extreme caution.

Alas, subsequent American denominations, in a land settled by a rather heterodox cohort an ocean away from the historic churches, with the exuberant triumphalism of a young republic that successfully separated itself from the crown and the Old World, cultivated a cavalier and flippant attitude toward tradition. As American culture seeped into the doctrine and practices of these churches like hotdog grease into a bun, they drifted away from the original Protestant vision, and away from Paul's vision of Church unity. The Non-denominational movement is the *reductio ad absurdum* of the trajectory of American Evangelicalism, with a patina of unity but no real ecclesiology, in which each church is a denomination unto itself, an island — sometimes complete with Hawaiian-shirt-wearing pastors. Meanwhile, the entire Evangelical subculture and media ecosystem has very much siloed itself off from the rest of Christendom.

A healthier disposition is one that seeks to stay true to the worship practices that for two millennia have been shared by all Christians — such as chanting the psalms, reciting the creeds, centering church services on communion, making the sign of the cross, singing hymns, etc. Preserving these practices is an expression of love for their inherent beauty, for the many generations of Christians who diligently bequeathed them to us, and for all the current Christians who continue unwavering.

Lest we forget, the vast majority of global Christians are Roman Catholic and Orthodox. The Anglican Communion, with approximately 85–97 million members worldwide, is one of the largest single formal Protestant denominational bodies,[^13] and along with Lutherans, one of the most intentional about maintaining kinship with the church catholic, despite particular doctrinal differences. We share the same liturgical calendar, and on any given Sunday, one could wander into a Roman Catholic, Orthodox, or Magisterial Protestant church and expect to hear the exact same appointed scripture readings from the Revised Common Lectionary.[^14]

In conclusion, for those who wish to strive for Paul's vision for Church unity, while maintaining the doctrinal integrity of the Reformation, the Anglican Church is a preferable option.

## 4. The Non-denominational Movement Has Structural Issues

Non-denominational churches are prone to two distinct defects: a reliance on individual personalities over institutions, and a potentially counterproductive evangelical strategy.

### The value of institutions, rather than individual personalities.

This is a philosophical point. Institutions form their members, providing them with roles greater than themselves. However, institutions can be captured and perverted into personal platforms by populist usurpers. This is a perennial problem in politics, when a cult of personality forms around a charismatic politician who is seen as greater than his/her office. A critical distinction here is between critiquing the corruption within an institution and opposing institutions *as such*. The Protestant Reformation was not anti-institutional — it sought to repair a corrupted institution and maintain it.

The Evangelical movement, however, has from its inception been anti-institutional and populist in nature. In the First Great Awakening, American Christianity began to absorb aspects of its cultural milieu — rugged individualism, reckless entrepreneurialism, no-frills practicalism, folk mistrust of elite society, celebrity worship, etc. — at an accelerated rate. George Whitefield, widely regarded as America's first celebrity,[^15] amassed a following as he impugned the faith of pastors at established churches. With class and education dynamics at work, Americans in the rough frontiers saw the animated, emotional, plainspoken preaching of the tent revivals as more "authentic" than the quiet, professorial pastors of the stuffy, institutional churches. This should all sound familiar. Soon, a dramatic story about one's "conversion experience" became a more important credential than a seminary degree. The end result is modern Non-denominational churches that are the personal platforms of dynamic pastors.

The distinction is exemplified in the clerical attire of Anglican priests, as opposed to Non-denominational pastors. The priesthood has a uniform — and it *is* uniform. The members strive to uphold a dependable standard, with a rigorous seminary and selection process for ordination, and a consistent liturgy. An Anglican sermon is generally shorter than one would expect to hear at a Non-denominational church. This centers the Eucharist, leaves time for the rest of the liturgy, and, exigently, leaves *less* time for speculation, focusing the sermon on doctrinal fundamentals. All of this amounts to quality control, by which one can be more or less assured to hear sound theology, consistent and catholic, from any given Anglican priest.

In a Non-denominational setting, however, the pastor is not uniformed, but rather dons the clothing that best fits his/her personal brand. The sprawling sermons heighten the necessity of performative dynamism (can I get an *amen*!?), and the effect is that one feels oneself more the follower of a pastor than the member of a church.

### The Non-denominational evangelical strategy may be counterproductive.

To be sure, Non-denominational churches appear to be flourishing, even as overall church attendance declines. The Evangelical movement prioritizes evangelizing over and even at the expense of the other considerations discussed above — historical rootedness, reverent worship, etc. So it would be natural to assume that evangelism is their core strength, the capacity they optimized for. The start-up nature of a Non-denominational church allows it to move nimbly, adapt, and experiment, crafting the right formula in any given context to put the most butts into chairs.

That being said, these short-term gains likely mask an immeasurable undercurrent of long-term losses. It is impossible to track how many souls have been completely repelled from Christianity by just minimal exposure to Evangelical preaching and theatrics. Surely the number is immense. The Roman Catholic Church suffered under generations of stigma after the sex abuse scandal; a stigma has also attached to Christianity as a whole, due to high exposure to the many elements of Evangelical eccentricity that permeate popular culture — political televangelists, prosperity-gospel charlatans, kitschy C.C.M., dinosaurs on Noah's ark, grave sucking and snake handling — the memes are endless. The worst examples are not representative of *all* Evangelicals, but there is a reason one rarely hears about such things coming from a traditional denomination. This project as a whole is corrosive to the credibility of Christianity in the wider culture, and for that reason the Anglican Church is a preferable option.

## 5. The Anglican Church is Historic

The Anglican Church possesses deep historic roots. This is compelling because, in a church that is steeped in history, the congregants are also more likely to be steeped in history, and because the Anglican Church has apostolic succession.

### Anglicans are steeped in history.

In a modern church, untethered from antiquity, with a worship service equally untethered from historic practices, one hardly expects to hear discussion of Christian history. But, while scripture is of primary importance, history is also indispensable in helping us understand who we are as Christians, that we might stand on the shoulders of theological giants. I have a civic duty as an American to possess at least a basic understanding of American history. How much more important then, should it be for me, as a Christian, to have a basic understanding of Christian history? If, as it ought, my religious identity precedes my national identity, then surely I should be as conversant in Thomas Aquinas as Thomas Jefferson, as familiar with the ecumenical councils as the enumerated powers, Constantinople and the Constitution (ok, I'm stretching it).

Just as the culture of a nation grows out of its history, so too with Christianity. When we participate in historic Christian practices, we propagate a distinctly Christian culture throughout the ages. When we abandon them, the void is filled by the ephemera of empty pop culture and consumerism. In the Anglican Church, by contrast, our connection to Christian history is implicit in our liturgy, and sometimes explicit in the sermon — a priest will occasionally quote Augustine or explain the historical context for something we do.

### Anglicans have apostolic succession.

Moreover, Anglicans are unique among Protestant denominations for maintaining the historic episcopate. Like the Roman Catholic and Orthodox churches, we have bishops whose lines of succession extend back through tradition to the apostles themselves. Though the genealogical records are imperfect, we know that the bishopric emerged extremely early in church history, having been ordained by the laying on of hands. Note that Timothy was given a special charism via the laying of hands in his ordination:

> Until I come, devote yourself to the public reading of Scripture, to exhortation, to teaching. Do not neglect the gift you have, which was given you by prophecy when the council of elders laid their hands on you. (1 Timothy 4:14–15)

Paul also warned Timothy to be selective in whom he ordains:

> Do not admit a charge against an elder except on the evidence of two or three witnesses. As for those who persist in sin, rebuke them in the presence of all, so that the rest may stand in fear. In the presence of God and of Christ Jesus and of the elect angels I charge you to keep these rules without prejudging, doing nothing from partiality. Do not be hasty in the laying on of hands, nor take part in the sins of others; keep yourself pure. (1 Timothy 5:19–23)

For historical context, the early Church warned its flock against following heretical groups like the Gnostics, and differentiated the true Church from these heretical churches by its apostolic lineage. Clement I, an early Bishop of Rome apparently in the line of succession after Peter, wrote this in c. 95 A.D.:[^16]

> Through countryside and city [the apostles] preached, and they appointed their earliest converts, testing them by the Spirit, to be the bishops and deacons of future believers. Nor was this a novelty, for bishops and deacons had been written about a long time earlier. . . . Our apostles knew through our Lord Jesus Christ that there would be strife for the office of bishop. For this reason, therefore, having received perfect foreknowledge, they appointed those who have already been mentioned and afterwards added the further provision that, if they should die, other approved men should succeed to their ministry. (*Letter to the Corinthians* 42:4–5, 44:1–3)

And here is Bishop Irenaeus (c. 125–202 A.D.):[^17]

> It is possible, then, for everyone in every church, who may wish to know the truth, to contemplate the tradition of the apostles which has been made known to us throughout the whole world. And we are in a position to enumerate those who were instituted bishops by the apostles and their successors down to our own times, men who neither knew nor taught anything like what these heretics rave about. (*Against Heresies* 3:3:1)

> But since it would be too long to enumerate in such a volume as this the successions of all the churches, we shall confound all those who, in whatever manner, whether through self-satisfaction or vainglory, or through blindness and wicked opinion, assemble other than where it is proper, by pointing out here the successions of the bishops of the greatest and most ancient church known to all, founded and organized at Rome by the two most glorious apostles, Peter and Paul… Polycarp also was not only instructed by apostles, and conversed with many who had seen Christ, but was also, by apostles in Asia, appointed bishop of the church in Smyrna, whom I also saw in my early youth, for he tarried [on earth] a very long time, and, when a very old man, gloriously and most nobly suffering martyrdom, departed this life, having always taught the things which he had learned from the apostles, and which the Church has handed down, and which alone are true. (*ibid.* 3:3:2–4)

It appears that the entrepreneurial impulse to simply found one's own church without regard for the apostolic Church was already a problem back then, as it is now. Of note, the historical consensus is that not all of the early churches had bishops — in some churches, a council of elders elected the presbyter. Thus, Anglicans do not typically argue that bishops are a necessity for a church, the way that Roman Catholics and Orthodox do (we merely contend that it is better to have them than not to).

The line of succession can exist even where bishops do not — elders and presbyters would have all received the laying on of hands as well. Thus, Lutherans, who do not have bishops, can still claim valid ordinations, because the first Lutheran ministers had all been ordained by Roman Catholic bishops, before the Lutheran church separated. Many argue that a special charism is passed down by the laying on of hands during the ordination process, as was the case with Timothy, by which the Holy Spirit enables the ordained minister to perform his role — delivering the sacraments, preaching the Word, pronouncing Christ's forgiveness for sins confessed, etc. Even if one does not hold to this position, it is evident that a tangible connection to the Church that Christ founded is of immense value. The Roman Catholic, Orthodox, Anglican, and (to maybe a lesser degree) Lutheran churches can claim this connection.

Other denominations contend that apostolic succession is really about the passing down of the apostles' writings — thus, anyone who has read the New Testament is in the "line of succession" and can be a pastor. This is certainly not what was originally meant by "apostolic succession." Still others will simply dismiss the whole concept. I take Pascal's wager here. Perhaps a direct line of succession is not strictly necessary, but given the option, I would much rather be in a church that can trace its lineage to the apostles than one that cannot.

## 6. Anglicans Baptize Infants

Scripture is not explicit on this topic, in one way or the other, but cogent reasons to believe in the doctrine of paedobaptism are plenty. Paul likened baptism to circumcision.[^18] Jews circumcised their sons at eight days of age and, based on Paul's words, would naturally assume that they should baptize infants. Paul, a Jew himself, knew this, and would have known to be perspicuous in repudiating this assumption. The widespread practice of infant baptism in the early Church suggests that it was no accretion but indeed part of the apostolic deposit.[^19] Consider the witness of Irenaeus, himself likely the child of Christian parents who probably baptized him as an infant, writing in c. 189 A.D.:

> [Jesus] came to save all through himself; all, I say, who through him are reborn in God: infants, and children, and youths, and old men. Therefore he passed through every age, becoming an infant for infants, sanctifying infants; a child for children, sanctifying those who are of that age… [so that] he might be the perfect teacher in all things, perfect not only in respect to the setting forth of truth, perfect also in respect to relative age. (*Against Heresies* 2:22:4)

Household baptisms occurred in Acts:

> One of those listening was a woman from the city of Thyatira named Lydia, a dealer in purple cloth. She was a worshiper of God. The Lord opened her heart to respond to Paul's message. When she and the members of her household were baptized, she invited us to her home. (Acts 16:14–16)

Note that the Lord opened *her* heart only, and then her *whole household* was baptized. Here is another example:

> The jailer called for lights, rushed in and fell trembling before Paul and Silas. He then brought them out and asked, "Sirs, what must I do to be saved?" They replied, "Believe in the Lord Jesus, and you will be saved — you and your household." Then they spoke the word of the Lord to him and to all the others in his house. At that hour of the night the jailer took them and washed their wounds; then immediately he and all his household were baptized. (Acts 16:30–33)

When God made His covenant with Abraham, as with other Old Testament figures, His covenant extended to his descendants. Just as Abraham was circumcised, so too were his male descendants. Likewise, when someone sins, the effects of said sin can ripple across generations. The Christian life entails a personal relationship with Christ, but it cannot be reduced to this. The family unit also has a relationship with Christ. And most fundamental is the relationship between Christ and His Church, His bride, His body. It is this covenant between Christ and His Church that we enter into in baptism.

These biblical notions of intergenerational covenants and community faith collide with inordinately individualistic, American sensibilities. Denominations (and non-denominations) with more individualistic theologies flourish here, highlighting the importance of the personal relationship with Christ at the expense of these corporate relationships.

In God's old covenant, the children, too, were His chosen people. The new covenant is more inclusive than the old. The scope broadened to include Gentiles and, where only men were circumcised before, both men and women are baptized in the new covenant. It would contradict this general principle of a more expansive and inclusive covenant if God were to suddenly exclude the children whom He had once included, inverting the trajectory of redemptive history. Consider that, before baptizing 3,000 people in Acts, Peter proclaimed the following:

> Repent and be baptized every one of you in the name of Jesus Christ for the forgiveness of your sins, and you will receive the gift of the Holy Spirit. For the promise is for you and for your children and for all who are far off, everyone whom the Lord our God calls to himself. (Acts 2:39–40)

As was the case with the old covenant, the new covenant is for the faithful *and their children*. God did not excommunicate the babies. We ought to reflect on what Jesus said about the faith of children:

> Whoever receives one such child in my name receives me, but whoever causes one of these little ones who believe in me to sin, it would be better for him to have a great millstone fastened around his neck and to be drowned in the depth of the sea. (Matthew 18:5–6)

This is of no small significance. While not the same as the mature and articulate faith of adults, a *proto-faith*, so to speak, exists in children, to be nurtured. Baptism provides prevenient grace, like a promissory note, until the child reaches the age at which he/she can profess his/her mature faith at confirmation.

> When children were brought to Jesus by their parents for his blessing, the disciples tried to brush them aside but Jesus said, "Permit the children to come to Me... for the kingdom of God belongs to such as these." Jesus took them in His arms and began blessing them. (Mark 10:13–16)

We should permit the children to come to Him, by baptizing them into Him. At the Anglican Church, we do, and for that reason the Anglican Church is a preferable option, for us and for our children.

## Conclusion

In conclusion, one ought to seek a church with true, sacramental doctrine, proper worship, an eye toward Christian unity, and historic roots, without the structural issues that beset the Non-denominational space. None of this is to say that one can't find Jesus, hear great preaching, attain deep biblical knowledge, and experience spiritual growth at a Non-denominational church. But the Anglican Church is a preferable option specifically for all of the above-listed factors. This is a lot to absorb at once, so take some time to reflect, pray on it, and recognize that I'm definitely right about everything.
